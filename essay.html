Benchmarks are Fossils

Akhil Pandey Akella

AllSci Corp, Sunwater Capital (aakella@allsci.com)

Abstract

Scientific benchmarks are often read as isolated scores, but their greater value is archival: each benchmark preserves a task world, a measurement protocol, and a set of assumptions about what counts as scientific capability. ScienceEval treats these records as fossils. Today, models are released, evaluations are run, and broad claims are made about general intelligence, usefulness, and scientific utility. Yet benchmark growth has followed model growth, making it difficult to know which evaluations remain informative, especially for science. Fossils-M narrows this question to a science-facing record spanning FrontierScience, GPQA, PubMedQA, BioASQ, BioRED, SciERC, MMLU, SimpleQA, and SciRIFF. Rather than ask whether a model is broadly capable, it asks how models perform across expert reasoning, biomedical question answering, relation extraction, general knowledge, factuality, and scientific instruction following. The central experiment is whether small models, when trained on one fossil such as SciRIFF, show measurable gains before post-train vs after post-training across the full Fossils-M field. If they do, a second question follows: is improvement a property of that dataset alone, or can training on any one fossil produce broader movement across the scientific benchmark record?
